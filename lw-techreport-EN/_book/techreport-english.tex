\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

The existence of a relationship between an individual's size and its weight is a central tenet in biology. While size is usually described as a one-dimensional measure (e.g.~height or length), weight is a measure of a three-dimensional process.

The study of the relationship between size and weight, allometry.

Observations of size and weights are obtained during a number of scientific activities, and their respective measurements are highly influenced by the tools used to obtain them. While height and length measurements are readily obtained using a ruler, weight measurements require a scale. The precision associated with weight measurements is directly related to the instrument used, and scales usually have a minimum weight that they can report. Additionally, the design of scales has changed over time, leading to more precise measurements over time.

In scientific monitoring programs such as annual marine ecosystem surveys conducted on research vessels, a large number of individuals of different marine taxa are measured and weighted. It is often the case that length measurements are available for all individuals caught, but weight measurements are only available from a subset of observations. Length-weight relationships are required to predict weight based on length observations. This is an essential analytical component of many stock assessments, since commercial landings and many reference points are in weight units but the underlying information collected are length measurements.

In the Gulf Region of Fisheries and Oceans Canada, an annual update of weight-at-size relationship parameters was historically done using a script written in SAS (\citeproc{ref-sas-citation}{SAS Institute Inc. 2026}). This script evolved over a number of years and accreted ad hoc steps to identify errors and treat different taxa differently based on the number of observations available. This script was considered as the authoritative source to estimate the weight-at-size parameters used in weight predictions.

We present a statistically sound framework to estimate length-weight relationship parameters, and apply it to observations of various fish and invertebrate taxa captured in the annual September ecosystem survey of the southern Gulf of St.~Lawrence.

A cohesive approach is used to obtain length-weight relationship parameter estimates for taxa caught in the annual southern Gulf of St.~Lawrence September survey. The approach utilises the number of available observations and the range of observed lengths and weights to determine where annual estimates can be derived with confidence. The sampling protocols used in the survey are incorporated in the framework so that sex-specific parameters can also be calculated when available data is available. When annual estimates can be meaningfully estimated, a single model is fitted to available observations and the parameter estimates are reported. The approach provides a robust and reproducible method that can be used to annually update length-weight relationship estimates.

\subsection{Allometric function}\label{allometric-function}

The weight-at-size relationship used is the following: \begin{equation}
W = \alpha L^{\beta}
\end{equation} where \(W\) is observed weight in grams, \(L\) is the length in centimeters, and \(a\) and \(b\) are the allometric parameters to be estimated. It is common to log transform this relationship to make it linear: \begin{equation}
log(W) = log(\alpha) +  \beta log(L)
\end{equation} The parameter \(log(\alpha)\) is referred to as the ``\(a\)'' parameter, and \(\beta\) is referred to as the ``\(b\)'' parameter.
Individual fish observations of length-weight pairs are used to estimate model parameters.

\subsection{Mixed effects models}\label{mixed-effects-models}

To allow for sex, size and year effects on the weight-at-size relationship, a mixed effects framework is adopted to analyse the available individual observations of length-weight pairs. The models considered appear in Table~\ref{tab:tablemodels}. Models are implemented using the \emph{glmmTMB} R package (\citeproc{ref-McGillycuddy-etal-2025}{McGillycuddy et al. 2025}) using R 4.6.0 (\citeproc{ref-cite-R}{R Core Team 2026}).

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

The values of \(a\) and \(b\) parameters are used to flag erroneous lengths and weights measurements during data collection.

The predicted weights obtained from the fitted model are used in computations of biomass indices based on individual length observations.

If the yearly changes in estimated values of \(a\) and \(b\) are auto-correlated, it may be indicative of gradual changes in condition. An alternative modelling approach that accounts for this auto-correlation should be investigated.

The availability of a sufficient number of samples is key to estimating a meaningful length-weight relationship.

The use of base 10 logarithm that was implemented in the SAS scripts does not provide any advantage when compared to using natural logarithm. The resulting length-weight relationship will be the same but its parameter estimates will have different values.

Froese et al. (\citeproc{ref-Froese-etal-2014}{2014})

The framework developed and presented here provides a defensible and appropriate method to obtain robust length-weight regressions model estimates. The predicted weights obtained from the fitted models leverage all available information while employing contemporary statistical methods.

The encapsulation of the methods within a script provides an elegant way to update these indices yearly, as new information becomes available.
